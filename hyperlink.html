ANNOTATIONS IN NARRATIVE VISUALIZATION

This is a framework for annotation recommendations for different audiences in narrative visualization. By Jonathan Nguyen

Recommendations

1. Image Hyperlinks

Screenshot - 1

Why do cats & dogs ...? Nadieh Bremer

In this example it uses moderately interactive rollovers as annotations in narrative visualization (see screenshot - 1). Only when the content is rolled over does the annotation appear. It appears always in the top left hand corner. When hovered over, the cursor turns into a pointer, indicating a clickable element. When clicked, it leads to a google search of the hovered content.

Recommendation

Use only for under 30 audience

Consider making hyperlinks open a new page, rather than replace the current page

DO NOT use for senior audience as it may overwhelm the user due to a different layout

3. Text Hyperlinks

Screenshot - 3

How many high school stars make it in the NBA?

The Pudding is a website that cater towards a primarily 18-24 year old demographic (Similar Web)

In this example, the narrative visualisation utilises an annotation at the very bottom regarding visualisation creation methodology and source. The hyperlink is underlined. When hovered over, the cursor turns into a pointer, indicating a clickable element. When clicked, it leads to the data source of the visualisation. An underline and/or bolding of text is often utilised to distinguish between content text and hyperlinked text. This works alongside a visual scheme to make a more unique and uniform website aesthetic.

Recommendation

Use for an audience older than 18 years old

Consider making hyperlinks open a new page, rather than replace the current page

Consider underlining the hyperlink when targeting a low internet literacy demographic
